Rules

  1. All rules of chess apply unless they conflict with the following rules.
  2. Before the game begins, each player secretly designates one of their rooks and one of their bishops as a cannon.
  3. Cannons move normally when not capturing, but capture by jumping over exactly one piece between themselves and the target piece. A cannon cannot capture without jumping and cannot jump without capturing.
  4. A piece whose status as a cannon or not is not revealed (e.g. a White bishop before White has made a capture with a bishop) may not give check or checkmate.

Commentary

The hidden information aspect makes the game highly psychological. It's possible to bluff — e.g. by placing a piece where it is "protected" only by a potential cannon that may or may not actually be protecting it — and to call the opponent's bluff, e.g. by capturing that piece. If you play multiple games in a row with the same opponent, there is also the "meta" aspect of deciding which rook and bishop to designate as cannons.

Rule 4 has a few interesting consequences, such as allowing a new type of tactic that might be called "revealed check". For example:

  1. e4 e5
  2. Nf3 Nc6
  3. Bb5 d6?
  4. d3 Nf6
  5. Bg5 h6??
  6. Bxf6+ gxf6
  7. Bxd8

(Incidentally, 5...h6 leads to losing the queen regardless of White's cannon designation. Either way White would capture a knight with check and then the queen!)

Rule 4 also implies the possibility — albeit unlikely — of a king-and-rook-versus-king endgame where the side with the rook is unable to give check, and therefore unable to give checkmate, because they never made a rook capture.

Of course, in addition to all this, there's the endless tactical patterns and possibilities — and potential endgames to explore, although a game of Hidden Cannon Chess may not often make it that far — introduced by having a piece with an entirely new move mechanic. Well, entirely new for Western Chess!
